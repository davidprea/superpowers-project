## The skills that matter most are the hardest to teach.

We call them Superpowers -- the uniquely human capacities that AI can't replace and schools have struggled hardest to measure.

The world is changing fast. The careers that AI can't take are the ones that depend on uniquely human capacities — creativity, perseverance, communication, critical thinking, collaboration, empathy. Different people call these by different names: durable skills, soft skills, 21st-century skills, transferable skills. We call them <strong>Superpowers</strong>.\*

Superpowers are what learners need most, and they are also what schools have struggled hardest to develop systematically. We know how they're built: through real work, with mentoring and feedback, over time. But assessing whether a student is actually growing in these skills requires reading their work carefully, against a framework, looking for evidence across many artifacts. That kind of assessment is labor-intensive. Multiply by a class, a school, a year, and it becomes impossible.

So Superpowers have remained aspirational in most schools: talked about in mission statements, too infrequently measured in practice. This project exists to close the gap between what we say we value and what we can actually do.

<small>\* *We borrow the Superpowers term from John Danner, founder of Flourish Schools.*</small>

## Real learning happens through iteration.

AI makes fast, specific feedback possible at scale -- turning one-shot assignments into the revision cycles that actually build skill.

Writers revise. Engineers prototype. Musicians rehearse. In every craft outside of school, getting good at something means doing it, getting feedback, doing it better, getting feedback again. School is the anomaly: students typically get one shot, receive a grade, and move on — not because that's how learning works, but because feedback at scale has been too expensive to provide any other way.

AI can change that. Not by replacing the teacher, but by serving as their assistant coach. The student initiates the loop and the teacher joins in, with both doing the hard cognitive work; the AI is a fast first reader and annotator, not a tutor. The teacher's role gets richer, not smaller. They see deeper drafts, give higher-leverage feedback, and come to know each student more fully through the work itself.

When iteration is built in, mistakes stop being verdicts and start being information. When the work is about iteration itself...not a "final" draft...students engage differently; they take on harder problems, try less safe approaches, and recover from missteps without losing ground. The fear that drives so much shallow work in school is largely a response to the one-shot, high-stakes model. Remove the constraint and the fear softens.

## When colleges read the work, schools can finally teach.

Move the judging out of the classroom and what's left is the relationship that makes real learning possible.

Today, colleges and employers rely on grades, rankings, and recommendations because they have no practical way to read what applicants have actually done. That compressed signal has shaped everything upstream: schools optimize for ranking and sorting, students strategize for the easiest credentials, parents push for impressive resumes rather than meaningful experiences.

AI-assisted portfolio review changes the calculus. When admissions offices can use their own skills framework to validate an applicant's body of work, applied identically to *all* applicants, the credential becomes unnecessary. The signal is the work. Grade inflation evaporates. Under-resourced schools no longer hand their students a discounted credential. Each institution can weigh what it actually cares about.

The deeper change happens upstream. When colleges read student work directly, schools no longer need to compete on rankings, and teachers are released from a contradiction they've carried forever: being asked to be play both advocate and judge. Mentors and judges can't be the same person — students know it, and teachers feel it. Move the judging out of the classroom and what's left is the relationship that makes real learning possible. That release is the gift this project ultimately offers to the people who chose teaching for the right reasons.
